Shear stress in an Icesheet

Purpose

The purpose of this task is to find the behaviour of the shear stress of an ice sheet under different conditions.

  1. Constant density, with shear stress as a function of depth (z).
  2. Variable density, (therefore shear stress as function of density (ρ) and depth (z)
    3. Where density is a function of pressure pressure is given by
  3. To find the depth of the fracturing level (where the stress (variable density) crosses 2 MPa) as a function of the slope angle.

Method

I have used MATLAB to do all the iterative calculations, store the calculations in systematic way and present the data as the relevant plot.
The basic format my code follows:

  1. Initialising variables (constants, zero matrices and columns to be filled, initialising matrices/vectors)
  2. Loops (to iterate calculations and store them in the respective matrices/vectors. May also use previous calculations.) with termination condition when all calculations are done.
  3. Graphing (plotting the variables, formatting graph legend, axes labels, ranges and titles)
I have two different codes:
  1. Calculating and Graphing shear stress with constant density and stress with variable density as a function of x and depth (m) as function of y superimposed on the same plot. Calculating the percent error of the constant density shear stress to variable density shear stress and plotting on a different figure.
    2. Initialization Loops (iterative calculations) The above uses the equations defined in the introduction. For constant density it directly calculates the shear stress with the constant density initialised in the previous part and appends it to an indexed value (z+1) in the respective vector. For variable density it first calculates the pressure, appends it to the respective vector, then uses this value to calculate the density. This variable density is then used to calculate shear stress which is stored in a different vector indexed by (z+1). Note: the shear stress also calls on the previous value to use in calculations. Then the error is calculated as the abs value of difference between the two shear stresses over the variable density times 100. The layer (z) is then incremented by 1 and loop repeats as long the z stays under 61. This ensures that the calculations happen till the depth of 3km.
    Graphs The z is redefined as a vector [0,3000] with increments of 50. Both the shear stresses are superimposed and plotted as functions of x against z on y axis. The axes are labeled, the graph titled and legend is added. On a different figure the error is graphed as function of y against z on the x axis. The ylim accommodates a range of [0,100] on the graph. X and y axes are labeled and the graph is titled.
  2. An adaptation of Code 1 but to calculate fracturing level depth as a function of slope angle and graph it.
    3. Initialization Note that the shear stress, pressure and density are a matrices of 16 columns now, to accommodate for the changing angle. Slope angle is a vector with values [5,20] with an increment of 5.
    Loops This time a for loop iterates for different angle values. Before starting the calculation, the shear stress is initialised. Then a for loop calculates shear stress for different layers with respective angles. Within the for loop is an if statement that checks if the stress is close to the fracturing level and its depth has not already been calculated (first value close to fracturing level).
    If the condition is true the if statement adds the layer depth to the resp matrix.
    Graphs Plots the angles (deg) on horizontal axis and resp depth on y axis (m). The plot is labeled and titled. The y axis is limited to a range of [2500,2700].

Results

  1. Constant density shear stress vs variable density shear stress (angle 10 deg)
    2. Fracturing level approx. 1300 m from graph
  2. Percent error of constant density shear stress with respect to variable density shear stress.
  3. Fracturing level depth vs slope angle

Discussions and Conclusions

We can notice from the trends that the magnitude of shear stress increases with the depth of the ice sheet, as the stresses of the previous layers add up. Since the stresses are added, it is no surprise that the trend is linear. It is also seen that when accounting compounding pressure and densities as the layers push down, the stress seems to increase at a higher rate. This is more realistic since the increasing mass of the ice above increases the downward weight force. As the area is still constant, pressure also increases. With increases pressure the ice becomes more compact and density increases, increasing the amount of stress acting on each particle due to smaller space.
As for slope and fracture level, it has been observed that increasing the angle makes the ice sheet more unstable, decreasing the depth at which fracturing occurs. Increasing the angle makes the direction of the normal force shift nearer to the ground in the direction of the slope. Therefore, this increases the resultant force downwards across the slope laterally, increasing the shear stress. Steeper the slope is, easier it is for the top layers of the ice sheet to slide down.
